# Your Personal Brand Sounds Like Everyone Else's. And You Have No Idea.

There's a test I run on every personal brand I review.

I take the headline. The bio. The "what I do" statement. Then I swap out the name.

Yours. Mine. A stranger's.

If it still makes sense with any name attached, it's not a brand. It's a template someone filled out.

Most people fail this test. Not because they're not smart. Not because they don't care. Because they built their brand by copying what already works, without the substance underneath.

---

## Borrowed Authenticity Is Still Theft

Everyone talks about being authentic.

That word has been stripped of meaning. It now signals the opposite of itself. "Authentic" in 2026 means you picked the right stock photo filter. You used lowercase. You ended your bio with something that sounds vulnerable but costs you nothing.

The real problem is subtler.

Most personal brands aren't fake. They're assembled. You copied someone's structure, tone, or way of telling a story.

The result is a brand that feels vaguely right and says nothing specific. A copy of a copy. Copies fade.

---

## Five Words Killing Your Brand

Look at your LinkedIn headline. How many of these appear: *passionate*, *results-driven*, *strategic*, *innovative*, *expert*.

These words aren't bad. They're irrelevant. Everyone in your space uses them. When everyone claims the same thing, the claim disappears.

Your brain ignores what's constant. That's why you stop hearing the refrigerator hum. That's why nobody reads bios that start with "I help."

The words most people use for their brand are that hum. Background noise. Gone before it arrives.

---

## You Built a Resume in a Blazer. Not a Brand.

Here's what happens when most people try to build a personal brand.

They list credentials. Add a point of view that sounds like something they read somewhere. Pick a niche safe enough not to alienate anyone. Post content that confirms what their audience already believes, because they're afraid of what happens if they say the thing they actually think.

That's not a brand.

A real personal brand costs you something. It makes someone uncomfortable. It draws a line. It says this is not for everyone, and I'm fine with that.

The moment you try to be for everyone, you stop being for anyone.

---

## You Already Know What Makes You Different

You just don't trust it yet.

It's probably the thing you rarely say out loud. The observation you soften before it leaves your mouth. The opinion you hedge because someone might push back.

That's the brand. Not the polished version. The one with a point of view sharp enough to cut.

Read your content and ask one question: could this have been written by someone else?

If yes, someone else already wrote it. You just don't know their name.

---

## Three Things. This Week. Not a 90-Day Rebrand.

**First:** Pull up your bio and remove every adjective that could describe anyone in your field. What's left? If nothing, you found the problem.

**Second:** Write down the one thing you believe about your industry that most people in it would disagree with. Not something edgy for the sake of it. Something you actually think and rarely say. That's your positioning.

**Third:** Find the last piece of content that made you slightly nervous before you hit post. That nervousness is signal. Go back there.

Your brand isn't built in the strategy session. It's built in the moments you stop softening the edges.
