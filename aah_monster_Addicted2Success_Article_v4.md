# Your Personal Brand Sounds Like Everyone Else's. You Just Don't Know It Yet.

Last month I went through 60 LinkedIn profiles of founders and executives. People who paid a designer for their banner. People who clearly thought hard about their headline. Real effort, real investment.

Twenty-six used the word "strategic." Nineteen used "passionate." Eleven used both. Three of them also used "thought leader" in the same sentence, which is the personal branding equivalent of showing up to a job interview and announcing that you're a hard worker.

Here's the thing that bothered me more than the word choices. I could swap out the names on 54 of those 60 profiles and nothing would break. Same person. Different font.

That's not a positioning problem. That's a more fundamental issue. Those people don't have a brand. They have a description of a job.


The Resume Disguised as a Personality

Most people approach it the same way. They look at someone who's built something real, something that makes you stop scrolling, and they reverse-engineer the surface. The tone. The cadence. The structure of how they introduce themselves. They copy the aesthetic of conviction without the actual conviction underneath.

I don't say this to be harsh. I did it too. My first LinkedIn bio read like a press release about a person I was trying to become. Confident-sounding sentences about expertise I genuinely had but had never figured out how to make specific. I knew my stuff. I just talked about it in a way that made me sound exactly like the 40 other people in my space who also knew their stuff.

The assembled version of you reads fine. It's technically accurate. It has the right keywords. But it's also forgettable in a way that's hard to diagnose, because nothing's obviously wrong with it.

You just blend in.


Why Safe Brands Die Quietly

I want to be careful here because "just be authentic" is possibly the most useless advice in personal branding, and I've heard it repeated so often it's started to mean the opposite of itself.

Authentic in 2026 means lowercase. It means a candid photo instead of a studio headshot. It means ending your bio with something that sounds vulnerable but that 300 other people on LinkedIn wrote last week.

The problem isn't inauthenticity. It's that most people borrow their authenticity from someone they admire. You see someone who communicates with real conviction and you absorb their cadence. You see someone whose story lands hard and you structure yours the same way. Nothing wrong with learning. But at some point the assembled version of you becomes the permanent version, and you stop asking what's actually yours.

The result: a brand that no one can disagree with, which means no one has a strong reason to choose you specifically.

A brand that can't be argued with isn't really a brand. It's a brochure.


Most LinkedIn profiles read like they were written by a committee trying not to get fired. That's not an accident. People optimize for safety when they don't know what they actually stand for. The hedged language, the non-committal positioning, the three bullet points of generic expertise. It's not dishonest. It's defensive.

And defensive brands attract no one in particular.


The Sentence You Keep Not Saying

You probably already know what makes you different. You've just been talking around it.

It's the take you always soften before you say it out loud, because you're not sure the room is ready for it. The specific experience you've had that genuinely changed how you see your industry, but that you've never written about because it feels too narrow or too honest or just too you. The thing you say to a client in a first meeting that makes them pause and go quiet for a second, because no one framed it that way before.

That's the brand. Not the polished, palatable, 160-character version of you. The version with a sharp edge.

Here's a test. Read your last ten posts, your bio, your about page, whatever you've got. Ask one question: could someone else in your field have written this? If yes, and I mean be honest here, someone else probably already did. You're running a cover version of someone else's original.

The version worth following is the one who says the uncomfortable thing clearly, because they actually believe it, not because they're performing controversy for the algorithm.


Three Things Worth Doing

I'm going to skip the part where I tell you this is a journey and personal branding takes time and consistency is key. You've read that version. It didn't help. Here's the short version instead.

Go through your bio and cut every adjective that could describe anyone in your industry. Results-driven. Passionate. Experienced. Out. What's left is either the start of something specific or confirmation that the work hasn't started yet.

Write down the one thing you believe about your field that would make someone at a conference uncomfortable if you said it directly. Not edgy for the sake of it. Something you've thought for a long time and mostly kept to yourself because it doesn't go over smoothly at industry events. That gap between what you actually think and what's politely accepted is where your positioning lives.

Find the last thing you published where you almost changed the ending before posting because it felt like too much. That hesitation is a signal. The thing you nearly pulled back is usually more interesting than everything else you've said this month.

Most people don't have a branding problem. They have a courage problem.

If your bio still makes sense after I replace your name with someone else's, that's where to start.
