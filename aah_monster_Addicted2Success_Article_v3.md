# Your Personal Brand Sounds Like Everyone Else's. And You Have No Idea.

There's a test I run on every personal brand I review. I take the headline, the bio, the "what I do" statement. Then I swap out the name. Yours. Mine. A stranger's.

If it still makes sense with any name attached, it's not a brand. It's a template someone filled out.

Most people fail this test. Not because they're not smart. Not because they don't care. They fail because they built their brand by studying what already works and then copied the feeling of it without the substance underneath. And the frustrating part is, they worked hard doing it. Hours on their bio. Weeks on their positioning. Real effort poured into something that still comes out looking like everyone else.

The good news is this is fixable. But first you have to see the problem clearly.


Borrowed Authenticity Is Still a Problem

Everyone talks about being authentic. The word has been so thoroughly stripped of meaning that it now signals the opposite of itself. In 2026, "authentic" means you picked the right filter, used lowercase, and ended your bio with something that sounds vulnerable but costs you nothing.

The real issue is subtler than faking it. Most personal brands aren't fake. They're assembled. You saw someone who communicates clearly and borrowed their structure. You saw someone with conviction and borrowed their tone. You saw someone with an interesting story and borrowed the way they told it.

None of those individual choices feel wrong in the moment. You're learning, you're iterating, you're trying things out. That's how anyone starts. The problem is when the assembled version becomes the permanent version, and you never go back to figure out what's actually yours.

The result is a brand that feels vaguely right and says nothing specific. It's a copy of a copy. And copies fade.


The Five Words Killing Your Brand

Go look at your LinkedIn headline right now. Count how many of these appear: passionate, results-driven, strategic, innovative, expert.

These words aren't wrong. They're just not doing anything for you. Every single person in your space has some version of that same sentence. When everyone claims the same qualities, the claims cancel each other out. The signal disappears into noise.

Your brain is wired to ignore what's constant. That's why you stop hearing the refrigerator hum after a few minutes. That's why nobody reads bios that start with "I help." Those words have been heard so many times they bypass attention entirely.

Most personal brand language works the same way. It's there, it's technically correct, and it registers as nothing. People scroll past it the same way they scroll past another post about Monday morning motivation.

The goal isn't to be louder. It's to be different enough that the brain can't ignore you.


A Resume in a Blazer Is Not a Brand

Here's what usually happens when someone decides to build their personal brand. They list their credentials. They add a point of view that sounds like something they read somewhere. They pick a niche that feels safe enough not to alienate anyone. Then they publish content that confirms what their audience already believes, because saying what they actually think feels risky.

That process produces a resume with a content strategy attached. It's not a brand. A brand requires a perspective that is genuinely yours, specific enough to be disagreed with, and consistent enough to be recognized over time.

A real personal brand costs you something. It makes someone uncomfortable. It draws a line somewhere and says this is not for everyone. That's not arrogance. That's clarity. And clarity is what makes people decide whether they want to work with you or follow you or trust you with their time.

The moment you try to be for everyone, you disappear. Not gradually. Instantly.


The Brand You're Sitting On

Here's the thing nobody tells you. You already know what makes you different. You just don't trust it yet.

It's the observation you keep softening before you say it out loud. The opinion you hedge in meetings because someone senior might push back. The experience you've had that genuinely shaped how you see your industry, but that you've never written about because it feels too specific or too honest or just too much like you.

That's the brand. Not the polished version. The one with a real point of view behind it.

There's a simple test for this. Read your last ten pieces of content, your last ten posts, your last ten conversations where you introduced yourself, and ask one question: could this have been said by someone else in your field? If yes, someone else probably already said it. You're just echoing.

The version of you that's worth following is the one who says the thing that nobody else in the room is willing to say. Not because it's edgy, but because it's true and you know it's true and you've been sitting on it long enough.


Three Practical Things You Can Do This Week

This doesn't need to be a six-month brand overhaul. Start here.

First, open your bio and delete every adjective that could describe anyone in your industry. Passionate, driven, experienced, expert. Remove all of it. What's left is either the beginning of something real or proof that the work needs to start over.

Second, write down the one thing you believe about your field that most people in it would push back on. Not something contrarian for the sake of attention. Something you actually think, that you've tested through experience, that you rarely say directly. That tension between what you believe and what's commonly accepted is where positioning lives.

Third, go back to the last piece of content you hesitated before publishing. The one where you almost softened the ending or changed the headline to something safer. That hesitation is a signal. The thing you almost said is usually more interesting than what you actually said.

Building a personal brand that works isn't about finding the right formula. It's about getting honest about what you actually think, finding the courage to say it clearly, and doing it consistently enough that people know what to expect from you.

That version of you is already there. It just needs to show up more often.
